## Predictive Coding and Active Inference

**Theory & Finding:**
Rao & Ballard (1999, "Predictive coding in the visual cortex: a functional interpretation of some extra-classical receptive-field effects") proposed that the brain implements a hierarchical predictive coding scheme where neurons at each level compare sensory input against predictions from higher levels, transmitting only prediction *errors* upward. Friston's Free Energy Principle (2010 onward, especially "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?") generalized this to a variational inference framework where the brain minimizes the divergence between its internal model and sensory reality—actively sampling the world to reduce uncertainty. Andy Clark's "Surfing Uncertainty" (2016) synthesizes these ideas for a general theory of embodied cognition: the brain is fundamentally a predictive machine that acts to make the world conform to its predictions.

**Testable Claim:**
Agents with prediction-error-driven learning should exhibit foresight by constructing hierarchical generative models: they predict future sensory states several steps ahead, compare predictions to reality, and update internal models. The agent becomes "conscious" of consequence (in an operational sense) when it can represent counterfactual predictions—what *would* happen if I did X—and choose actions that minimize surprise. This predicts that lesions to prediction-error circuits should impair future planning.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Prediction horizon depth:** How many steps ahead does the model predict? (H steps; cortical activity supports cascading predictions across timescales from 100 ms to several seconds)
- **Error threshold for model updating:** Threshold prediction-error magnitude above which synaptic weights are modified
- **Precision-weighting:** Higher sensory precision (lower sensory noise) should upweight sensory evidence relative to predictions; precision is encoded in gain modulation of prediction-error neurons (Feldman & Friston, 2010)

## Hippocampal Replay and Two-Stage Memory Consolidation

**Theory & Finding:**
Buzsáki's work on sharp-wave ripples (2015, "Hippocampal sharp wave-ripple: A cognitive tool for neuroscientists") describes a high-frequency oscillatory state (ripples, 150–200 Hz) during which hippocampal place-cell ensembles reactivate sequences of recent experience in compressed form—sometimes forward, sometimes backward. Stickgold's sleep-dependent consolidation framework (2005, "Sleep-dependent memory consolidation," Nature Reviews Neuroscience) shows that offline replay during sleep moves memories from hippocampus to cortex, integrating new memories with existing knowledge. Walker & Stickgold (2006, "Sleep, memory and plasticity") emphasize that sleep isn't just memory transfer; it's *selective*—high-prediction-error or emotionally salient sequences get more replay.

**Testable Claim:**
A foresight system must perform offline planning: after experiencing a sequence, the agent must reactivate those sequences in imagination (replay), compress them, and extract generalizable rules. Sharp-wave ripples mark the neural signature of this offline planning phase. Agents without replay should fail to consolidate long-term strategies and should make slower learning progress on goal-directed tasks; animals with disrupted ripples (pharmacologically or optogenetically) show impaired spatial and fear-extinction learning.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Replay compression ratio:** Sequences are replayed 5–20× faster than real-time. In ML, this is the ratio of imagined (offline) steps to real experience steps.
- **Replay selectivity:** High-surprise sequences (large prediction errors) are replayed ~3× more frequently than low-surprise sequences (Pfeiffer & Foster, 2013).
- **Consolidation timescale:** 4–6 hours for short-term hippocampal memory to begin transfer to cortex; full integration takes days.

## Hobson's AIM Model of Dreaming

**Theory & Finding:**
Hobson & McCarley (1977) and Hobson (2009, "REM sleep and dreaming: towards a theory of protoconsciousness") propose that dreaming arises from *brainstem-driven activation* (cholinergic) combined with *random input* and *modulatory state changes*. During REM sleep, the brain is activated (high firing rates) but receives no organized sensory input and has reduced norepinephrine and serotonin. Dreams are the brain's *attempt to make sense of incoherent internal activation*—a constraint-satisfaction process. Waking cognition is by contrast controlled (goals shape attention) and grounded in sensory reality.

**Testable Claim:**
Dreaming is not prediction-error minimization but *generative exploration* under constraint: the model generates plausible sequences without grounding them in reality. This produces "bizarre" dream content (sudden scene shifts, impossible physics). Compared to waking planning, dreaming should explore a larger space of possible futures and extract statistical regularities. Agents with a dream phase should show better generalization to novel scenarios than those with replay alone.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Activation level difference:** REM sleep maintains cortical firing rates ~90% of waking levels despite loss of sensory input.
- **Input gating reduction:** Sensory thalamic input to cortex is ~10× attenuated during REM.
- **Modulatory neuromodulator levels:** Norepinephrine and serotonin drop to ~50% of waking levels during REM. In ML: a "temperature" parameter or learning-rate multiplier during dream phases.

## Tononi & Cirelli Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis (SHY)

**Theory & Finding:**
Tononi & Cirelli (2003, "Sleep and synaptic homeostasis: a hypothesis"; 2013, "Sleep and the price of plasticity," Neuron) propose that learning during waking causes synaptic potentiation globally—the brain cannot remain in a constantly potentiated state. Sleep solves this by *downscaling all synapses proportionally* while *preserving relative strengths*. This allows the brain to reset metabolic capacity for the next day's learning while keeping learned information encoded in the relative structure of the network.

**Testable Claim:**
A system without homeostatic downscaling should accumulate synaptic noise, show progressively more chaotic internal dynamics, and require increasingly large prediction errors to drive learning (signal-to-noise collapse). Sleep-deprived agents show memory consolidation deficits not because memories aren't stored, but because *subsequent learning capacity degrades*.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Downscaling factor:** During sleep, synapses are reduced by ~15–20% in strength on average (Vyazovskiy & Tononi, 2013).
- **Selectivity threshold:** Weak synapses (below ~20% of maximum strength) are eliminated more frequently; strong synapses preserved.
- **Sleep duration:** 6–8 hours for full homeostatic reset in humans; 2–4 hours in mice.

## Panksepp's Seven Primary Affective Systems

**Theory & Finding:**
Jaak Panksepp (2004, "Affective Neuroscience"; 2012, "The Archaeology of Mind") identified seven evolutionarily conserved neural systems mediating fundamental motivations, present in mammals from rats to humans:

1. **SEEKING** (dopaminergic, VTA + NAcc): curiosity, exploration, goal-pursuit. Activated by uncertainty.
2. **FEAR** (amygdala, PAG): avoidance, freezing, escape. Latency ~50–100 ms in rodents.
3. **PLAY** (dorsolateral PAG, motor cortex): boisterous interaction, learning through practice. Suppressed by stress/predation threat.
4. **CARE** (anterior cingulate, insula): nurture, attachment. Oxytocin-mediated.
5. **RAGE** (hypothalamus, amygdala): defensive aggression when escape is blocked.
6. **PANIC/GRIEF** (anterior insula, dACC): separation distress.
7. **LUST** (hypothalamus, PAG): sexual motivation.

**Testable Claim for SEEKING:**
SEEKING is the primary driver of curiosity and active exploration. Dopamine doesn't encode reward value; it encodes *prediction error* about future rewards (Schultz, 2002). A model without SEEKING is passive (reactive to immediate stimuli) and unable to explore systematically.

**Testable Claim for FEAR:**
FEAR enables rapid switching to avoidance. The amygdala learns aversive associations in a single trial (unlike reward learning, which is gradual). A model without fast FEAR learning slowly adapts to threats — lethal in a predatory environment.

**Testable Claim for PLAY:**
PLAY is where the agent learns *motor control*, *social prediction*, and *latent abstract structures* without survival pressure. A model trained only on reward/punishment (SEEKING + FEAR) performs worse on high-dimensional motor tasks than one that includes a PLAY phase.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Affect intensity:** 0–1 scale per system; measured via freezing time, ultrasonic vocalizations, latencies.
- **Temporal dynamics:** FEAR fast (50–100 ms); SEEKING sustained (seconds–minutes); CARE gradual (minutes).
- **Cross-system inhibition:** FEAR suppresses PLAY and SEEKING. Quantify as gain reduction:
  `active_PLAY = PLAY_drive × (1 − λ × FEAR_intensity)`, λ ∈ [0,1] (Panksepp, 2004).

## Damasio's Somatic-Marker Hypothesis

**Theory & Finding:**
Damasio (1994, "Descartes' Error"; 1996) proposes that *decision-making is embodied simulation*. When the agent contemplates an action, the insula and ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) reactivate the *somatic state* (bodily/autonomic response) that would accompany that outcome — a "somatic marker." This guides choice: options with aversive bodily predictions are avoided.

**Testable Claim:**
A foresight model must maintain a *mapping from predictions to bodily/affective states*. When simulating future consequences, predict the *affective response* (FEAR, SEEKING activation), not just sensory/motor outcomes. Patients with vmPFC damage show intact logical reasoning but impaired real-world decision-making.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Somatic prediction latency:** ~300–500 ms in humans; ~100–200 ms in rodents.
- **Marker persistence:** seconds to minutes (chronic stress: days).
- **Embodiment depth:** dimensionality of body-state vector (temperature, heart rate, muscle tension, hormone levels).

## Tolman's Cognitive Maps and O'Keefe's Place Cells

**Theory & Finding:**
Tolman (1948, "Cognitive maps in rats and men") proposed animals learn *spatial maps*, not just stimulus-response associations. O'Keefe (1971) discovered place cells: hippocampal neurons that fire in specific locations. O'Keefe & Nadel (1978, "The Hippocampus as a Cognitive Map") synthesized: the hippocampus encodes a *topological model* of space supporting flexible planning.

**Testable Claim:**
A foresight model must construct a *latent spatial/state-action map* in which it can mentally simulate trajectories. Hippocampal lesions spare single-trial learning but abolish flexible navigation (e.g., latent place learning: rats learn maps without reward, then use them when motivation arises). The learned latent space should compress sensory input into a low-dimensional state representation where distance reflects behavioral similarity.

**Quantitative Parameters:**
- **Map dimensionality:** Hippocampal place fields ~10–40 cm in rodents.
- **Remapping plasticity:** Place cells reorganize fields when environment changes (rate remapping) but retain global structure.
- **Planning horizon:** Hippocampal ripples compress ~100 m of virtual space into ~100 ms (Pfeiffer & Foster, 2013).

## Tinbergen's Four Questions

Niko Tinbergen (1963) framed four classes of scientific questions about behavior:

1. **Mechanism (Causation):** What neural circuit/algorithm produces foresight in real time?
2. **Ontogeny (Development):** How does foresight emerge during an individual's development?
3. **Function (Adaptive Value):** What selective advantages does foresight confer?
4. **Phylogeny (Evolution):** How did foresight capability evolve across species?

A biologically grounded model must address all four. Mechanism = our architecture; ontogeny = our training curriculum; function = our benchmark task selection; phylogeny = our ablation logic (start minimal, add components, see what each unlocks).

## von Uexküll's Umwelt

von Uexküll (1957) introduced *Umwelt*: the species-specific perceptual world determined by an agent's sensory and motor capabilities. A tick's Umwelt comprises three stimuli (butyric acid, temperature, tactile pressure); a human's Umwelt is vastly richer. *The Umwelt is not the objective world*, but the world as structured by what the agent can sense and do.

**Implications:**
Consequence reasoning is always relative to an *embodiment*. A model that predicts sensory outcomes for actions assumes a particular body. Transfer requires retraining or reweighting. In ML: the world model must be jointly trained with the agent's embodiment; prediction errors are *relative to this agent's sensors and actuators*.

## Open Empirical Questions

1. **Affective prediction in latent space:** ML world models predict pixel-level consequences but not *affective state* (which system activates, at what intensity). Can we identify neural signatures (ventral striatum, anterior insula) for discrete affect-prediction dimensions?
2. **Replay selectivity mechanisms:** What circuit determines *which* sequences get replayed? High-surprise sequences get more replay, but the gating mechanism (dopamine? prediction error? task relevance?) is debated.
3. **Dream-phase function vs SHY:** Hobson's AIM (generative exploration) vs Tononi's SHY (homeostatic downscaling) — when does each dominate? Do early-sleep dreams (mostly SWS) differ functionally from late-sleep dreams (mostly REM)?
4. **Cross-embodiment transfer of foresight:** How much can a model trained in one body transfer to another? Damasio's markers are body-specific; Tolman's maps are space-specific.
5. **Primary affect system interaction in planning:** Can we decompose value functions into primary-system contributions and recover emergent behavior (e.g., risk aversion under threat) from first principles?

## Recommendations for an ML Implementation

1. **Implement prediction error as the core learning signal.** Hierarchical predictive coding; precision-weight prediction errors by sensory uncertainty (Feldman & Friston). Enables active inference.
2. **Add a dedicated sleep/offline phase with high replay compression.** Sharp-wave-ripple-like dynamics: high-prediction-error trajectories replayed at 5–20× speed. Prioritize by surprise (Pfeiffer & Foster; Stickgold).
3. **Apply synaptic homeostasis during sleep.** Multiplicative downscaling of network weights, ~15–20% per cycle (Tononi & Cirelli). Prevents saturation; mitigates catastrophic forgetting.
4. **Decompose the value function into primary affective systems.** Separate value estimates for SEEKING, FEAR, PLAY. Combine via weighted sum or multiplicative gating with cross-system inhibition (FEAR suppresses PLAY).
5. **Implement somatic-marker augmentation.** Predict *affective state* alongside sensory state; use markers as a fast heuristic to discount aversive trajectories (Damasio).
6. **Learn a latent spatial/relational map.** Train a latent encoder where distance reflects behavioral causality. Plan in latent space; decode back to sensory predictions (Tolman, O'Keefe).
7. **Implement a dream-generation phase with reduced sensory coupling.** Temperature parameter: low during waking (likelihood-faithful), high during dreaming (improbable-but-plausible). Hobson's AIM analogue.
8. **Cascade predictions across multiple timescales.** Hierarchy of predictors at 100 ms, 500 ms, 2 s, 10 s. Use scale-specific prediction errors to prioritize learning.
9. **Frame embodiment as a latent hyperparameter.** Predictions are conditioned on an implicit embodiment. Cross-embodiment transfer requires an adapter, not retraining (von Uexküll).
10. **Use Tinbergen's four questions to validate.** Mechanism: ablate prediction-error or sleep, observe degraded foresight. Ontogeny: bootstrap-from-naive curriculum. Function: benchmark tasks that *require* foresight. Phylogeny: minimal version (HPC + striatum, no cortex) for navigation; cortical layers for abstract planning.
